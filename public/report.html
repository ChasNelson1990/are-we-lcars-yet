Every claim on the compare page, sourced.

This is the citation trail behind the comparison table: real on-screen requests made of each era's computer, hologram, or holodeck system, what happened, how long it took when the show actually said, and whether the comparison page's specific claims hold up against primary transcripts and reference sources.

Coverage isn't even — some tiers got a deep pass, others a thin one, and that's marked honestly per section rather than padded out. See Method & Sources for what this does and doesn't cover.

NX-01 2151 · ENT

NX-01 runs on manual switches and dials with no touchscreen bridge interface. Its defining trait, repeatedly and on screen, is a universal translator that doesn't work — which is why Hoshi Sato is a main character. No holodeck exists anywhere aboard; the only holographic technology the crew encounters is explicitly alien. A later routine-usage pass also confirmed NX-01 has no food replicator: the galley uses a food cabinet/dispenser plus actual cooking by "Chef," supplemented by a hydroponic greenhouse and a protein resequencer for some items.

USS Discovery 2256–2258 · DISCO S1–2

Pre-Zora Discovery: a standard Starfleet computer plus the mycelial network, a navigation medium for the spore drive — not a data-storage system, despite how it's sometimes described. The ship briefly has holo-communicators; a malfunction gets them stripped out fleet-wide, which is the direct, on-screen reason Strange New Worlds has none. This pass also confirmed that no clean routine bridge-diagnostic moment could be sourced for this tier — recap sites systematically skip non-dramatic computer usage, which is itself a structural bias in how this fandom's secondary sources get written, not a gap unique to this project's research.

USS Enterprise (Pike refit) 2259–2261 · SNW

SNW's Enterprise computer is deliberately built to feel older than Discovery's own season 2, not newer — a real, on-screen technological regression (see the Discovery-early section above), not an authoring inconsistency. Its holodeck-precursor debut is framed by the show's own writers as the in-universe origin of the concept. This pass also confirmed SNW's food-service tech is termed "matter synthesizers," not replicators, and found routine computer moments genuinely scarcer here than on other tiers — a property of the show's verbal, non-visual interface and its recap coverage, not a research gap.

Constitution Class 2265 · TOS

The best-supported tier in this whole report. Its two headline traits — literal-mindedness and vulnerability to logical paradox — are quotable, on screen, twice over: Norman and Nomad both short-circuit on contradiction, in almost exactly those words. TOS also had the strongest notable-incident bias of any tier: a routine-usage pass read 11 full episodes hunting for boring computer moments, and 6 of them had none at all — TOS's computer genuinely is deployed for drama far more than for background texture.

Galaxy Class 2364 · TNG

Where the comparison page's "handles sarcasm reasonably well" grade doesn't match the transcripts: TNG's two biggest computer-driven plots — Moriarty and the Leah Brahms hologram — both start with the computer taking an instruction too literally, the same failure mode as TOS. TNG is also the pilot tier for correcting a sampling bias in this whole report: the first pass found eight incidents and every one was dramatic, because that's what search results surface. A second pass deliberately read full, unremarkable episodes instead of searching for "interesting" moments, and found six routine, nothing-went-wrong requests to sit alongside them — see Method & Sources.

Real World — Open Source 2026 · Framework + Fairphone + Open-Weight LLMs

Also not canon, also exempt from the episode-citation methodology. This tier represents the open, repairable, privacy-first strategy: open-weight models run local-first on hardware you can open with a screwdriver, plus paid E2E-encrypted services with open-source clients (Proton, Signal). The dividing line from the closed column isn't price — both columns spend real money — it's who holds the keys and whether you can read the code. Hosted open-weight API endpoints exist and are cheap, but they count here only as a caveat: the moment your tokens leave the building, you're back to trusting someone else's datacenter.

Method & sources

  • Primary sources: the chakoteya.net full-episode transcript archive (covers ENT, TOS, TNG, DS9, VOY) and Memory Alpha, the Star Trek wiki, cross-checked against episode recaps for Discovery/Picard/SNW, which chakoteya.net doesn't cover.
  • Every tier now has a deep research pass with notable-vs-routine tagging throughout (9–17 verified instances) — NX-01, Discovery (early, S1–2), SNW, TOS, TNG, DS9, VOY, Picard, and Discovery (Zora class, S3+). This grew in two stages: an initial pass left five tiers thin (1–3 instances, no notable/routine distinction on any tier), then a dedicated routine-usage pass — reading full, not-already-famous episodes instead of searching for "interesting" moments — was applied to every tier in turn, including the four (TNG, TOS, VOY, NX-01) that were already deep but still 100% notable-incident-sourced beforehand. TOS is the starkest result: 6 of 11 episodes read specifically to find routine computer usage had none at all, a real property of that tier, not a research gap. Coverage depth still isn't identical across tiers (some episodes simply have thinner secondary-source coverage than others, honestly reflected in each section's coverage note), but every tier's ratio of notable-to-routine citations is now visible rather than hidden inside a flat instance count.
  • Two technical snags came up during research and were resolved: a WebSearch session rate limit (affecting ENT and TOS, resolved on retry) and a chakoteya.net TLS certificate handshake failure (worked around by fetching over plain HTTP).
  • "Time taken" is reported only when an episode states or clearly depicts a duration on screen; most requests genuinely have no stated time — that's an accurate reflection of the source material, not a data gap.
  • This is not an exhaustive census of every computer interaction across nine series — it's a citation-backed sample chosen for variety and verifiability, not completeness. Coverage grows over time through more targeted research passes, not a bulk transcript pipeline (see the scraping note below for why).
  • Notable-incident bias: search-driven research finds Moriarty before it finds the fifty boring "Computer, time to arrival?" exchanges, because only incidents get written about. Left uncorrected, that skews every tier toward dramatic malfunctions and away from ordinary, nothing-went-wrong usage. Mitigation, applied going forward and first piloted on the TNG tier: pick a few episodes not already famous for computer-related plots, read each full transcript rather than searching for keywords, and require a minimum quota of routine requests per pass, not just notable ones. Each catalogued request below is tagged Notable or Routine so the ratio — and any remaining skew — stays visible instead of hidden inside a flat instance count.
  • Why there's no bulk pipeline: chakoteya.net's and st-minutiae.com's robots.txt files both explicitly disallow AI crawlers by name (GPTBot and others), even though the specific transcript directories used here aren't blocked for generic crawlers. Automated, systematic harvesting of hundreds of pages is exactly what that's aimed at, so this project doesn't do it — every citation above came from an individually-fetched page for a specific research question, the same access pattern a human reader or search engine already uses.
  • Verdict definitions: Supported a specific scene or line backs the claim · Partially supported the general idea holds but a detail is off, unattributed, or overstated · Not supported contradicted by, or absent from, the source material · Can't verify plausible but no citable on-screen source found.
  • Some Memory Alpha citations above link to the article's standard, predictable wiki address for a page the research explicitly named; not every individual link was re-verified live at publication time.
